Most habit advice fails for the same reason most diets fail: it asks for too much, too soon. We resolve to meditate for twenty minutes, run three miles, or journal every night — and for about four days, we do. Then life gets in the way, we miss once, and the whole thing quietly collapses.

**The problem was never your willpower.** It was the size of the ask. Behavior science has a stubbornly consistent finding: the smaller the habit, the more likely it is to survive contact with a real, messy week.

## Start smaller than feels reasonable

The trick is to shrink the habit until it's almost embarrassing. Not "do 20 push-ups" but "do one push-up." Not "read a chapter" but "read one page." A tiny habit is small enough that you can do it even on your worst day — and that reliability is the entire point.

<Callout icon="💡" title="The two-minute rule" tone="green">
  If a new habit takes more than two minutes to start, it's still too big.
  Shrink it until starting is effortless — you can always do more once you've
  begun.
</Callout>

Once the habit is small, the goal shifts from **performance** to **consistency**. You're not trying to get fit in a week; you're trying to become the kind of person who shows up. The reps come later, and they come easily, because you're no longer negotiating with yourself every single day.

## Anchor it to something you already do

New habits need a home. The most durable ones piggyback on routines that already run on autopilot — a phenomenon researchers call _habit stacking_. You don't have to find time; you borrow it from a habit you already keep.

- After I **pour my morning coffee**, I write down one thing I'm grateful for.
- After I **brush my teeth**, I read one page of a book.
- After I **close my laptop**, I take a two-minute walk around the block.

## Make the reward immediate

Behavior that gets rewarded gets repeated — but the reward has to arrive _now_, not in six months when you're finally fit or well-read. That's why the satisfying check, the growing streak, and the gentle celebration matter so much. They turn a future benefit into a present one.

<Callout icon="🕊" title="Never miss twice" tone="accent">
  Missing once is an accident. Missing twice is the start of a new habit. This
  is exactly why Pip gives you a grace token instead of resetting your streak to
  zero — one bad day should never undo a good month.
</Callout>

None of this requires heroic discipline. It requires a system that's kind enough to survive your worst weeks and satisfying enough to enjoy on your best ones. That's the whole idea behind Pip — and it's backed by decades of behavior research, not vibes.
